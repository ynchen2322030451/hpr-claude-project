================================================================================
Supplementary Note A: BNN training details
================================================================================

A.1. Evidence lower bound (ELBO) derivation
-------------------------------------------

The BNN posterior over weights w is approximated by a mean-field
variational distribution q(w) = product_i N(w_i | mu_i, sigma_i^2),
where each weight w_i has its own variational mean mu_i and variance
sigma_i^2. The variational parameters are optimised by minimising the
negative evidence lower bound:

  L(mu, sigma) = KL[q(w) || p(w)] - E_{q(w)}[log p(D | w)]

where p(w) = N(0, sigma_p^2 I) is the isotropic Gaussian prior and
p(D | w) is the data likelihood. The KL divergence between two
Gaussians admits a closed form; the data-fit term is approximated by a
single Monte Carlo sample per mini-batch (Bayes-by-Backprop [10]).

The reparameterisation trick enables gradient computation:
w = mu + sigma * epsilon, where epsilon ~ N(0, I). The variance
parameter sigma is parameterised as sigma = log(1 + exp(rho)) to
ensure positivity, with rho_abs_min clamping rho >= rho_abs_min to
prevent posterior collapse.


A.2. Heteroscedastic observation noise
--------------------------------------

The network outputs both a mean mu(theta) and a log-variance
log sigma^2_obs(theta) per output channel. The per-output Gaussian
log-likelihood is:

  log p(y_j | theta, w) = -0.5 * [log sigma^2_{obs,j} +
                           (y_j - mu_j)^2 / sigma^2_{obs,j}]

This heteroscedastic formulation allows the BNN to model input-
dependent observation noise, separating it from epistemic (weight)
uncertainty.


A.3. KL annealing schedule
--------------------------

The KL weight beta is annealed from 0 to its target value kl_weight
= 1.93 x 10^{-4} over the first 100 epochs using a linear schedule:

  beta(t) = min(t / 100, 1.0) * kl_weight

This prevents the KL term from dominating early training before the
data-fit term has found a reasonable region of weight space.


A.4. Physics-prior monotonicity constraints
--------------------------------------------

The physics-regularized BNN adds an auxiliary loss term that penalises
violations of known monotonicity relationships derived from the
constitutive laws. For each specified (input, output) pair where the
physics dictates a positive (or negative) partial derivative, the
loss is:

  L_mono = (1/K) sum_{k=1}^{K} max(0, -sign * d_mu/d_theta_k)

where the gradient is computed by automatic differentiation and K is the
number of sampled training points per batch (controlled by mono_topk).
The monotonicity loss is weighted by w_mono = 0.212 in the total loss.

The sign constraints are derived from the constitutive physics:
- Stress increases with E_intercept (positive sign), because
  sigma = C(E, nu) * epsilon and C is linear in E.
- Stress increases with alpha_base (positive sign), because thermal
  strain epsilon_th = alpha * DeltaT serves as the loading source.

These are physics-prior constraints, not inequality priors. The
inequality-constraint variant (tested in ablation, Supplementary
Table S3) is a separate formulation.


A.5. Gradient clipping and initialisation
-----------------------------------------

Gradient norms are clipped at 0.674 per parameter group. Weight means
mu are initialised with Xavier uniform; rho values are initialised
uniformly in [-5, -4], corresponding to small initial variance. These
choices stabilise early training when the KL term is annealed in.


================================================================================
Supplementary Note B: Sobol sensitivity methodology
================================================================================

B.1. Sobol variance decomposition
----------------------------------

For a square-integrable function f(theta_1, ..., theta_d) with
independent inputs, the total variance V = Var[f] can be decomposed as:

  V = sum_i V_i + sum_{i<j} V_{ij} + ... + V_{1,...,d}

where V_i = Var[E[f | theta_i]] is the first-order variance due to
theta_i alone, and higher-order terms capture interactions. The
first-order Sobol index is S_i = V_i / V, and the total-order index is
S_{T,i} = 1 - V_{~i} / V, where V_{~i} = Var[E[f | theta_{~i}]] is the
variance explained by all inputs except theta_i.


B.2. Saltelli cross-matrix scheme
----------------------------------

Indices are estimated using the Saltelli sampling scheme. Two
independent N x d matrices A and B are drawn from the joint prior. For
each input i, a cross-matrix A_B^{(i)} is constructed by replacing the
i-th column of A with the i-th column of B. The model is evaluated at
all N(d+2) points. First-order and total-order indices are then
estimated from the resulting output vectors.


B.3. Jansen estimator
----------------------

The first-order index is estimated using the Jansen estimator:

  S_1,i = 1 - (1/2N) sum_{j=1}^{N} [f(A_B^{(i)})_j - f(B)_j]^2 / V

The total-order index uses:

  S_{T,i} = (1/2N) sum_{j=1}^{N} [f(A)_j - f(A_B^{(i)})_j]^2 / V

The Jansen estimator is preferred for its lower variance compared to
the Sobol estimator, particularly for indices near zero.


B.4. Replication-based confidence intervals
-------------------------------------------

The entire Sobol analysis (sample generation, model evaluation, index
estimation) is independently repeated R = 50 times. The 90% confidence
interval for each index is constructed from the 5th and 95th percentiles
of the R replications. A factor is declared a stable contributor only if
the lower bound of its 90% CI exceeds zero.

The BNN posterior predictive mean (averaged over M = 30 weight samples)
serves as the deterministic function f(theta) in the Sobol framework.
Using the predictive mean rather than a single weight sample ensures
that the sensitivity analysis captures parametric sensitivity rather
than BNN sampling noise.


================================================================================
Supplementary Note C: MCMC posterior calibration details
================================================================================

C.1. Likelihood formulation
----------------------------

The likelihood for a single benchmark case with observed outputs
y_obs = (y_1, ..., y_J) is:

  log p(y_obs | theta) = sum_{j=1}^{J} log N(y_{obs,j} | mu_j(theta),
                          sigma_{obs,j}^2 + sigma_{BNN,j}^2(theta))

where mu_j(theta) is the BNN posterior predictive mean (averaged over
M = 20 weight samples), sigma_{BNN,j}^2 is the BNN aleatoric variance,
and sigma_{obs,j}^2 is the observation noise variance (2% of the true
value). The J = 5 primary outputs are: coupled peak global stress,
coupled peak fuel temperature, coupled peak monolith temperature,
coupled wall expansion, and k_eff.


C.2. Proposal distribution
---------------------------

A symmetric Gaussian random-walk proposal is used:

  theta* = theta_current + epsilon, epsilon ~ N(0, Sigma_proposal)

where Sigma_proposal = diag(scale_1^2, ..., scale_4^2). The proposal
scales are set to produce acceptance rates in the range 0.2-0.7 for
each parameter. The scales were calibrated by short pilot runs of 1000
iterations.


C.3. Chain configuration
-------------------------

For each benchmark case:
- 4 independent chains with dispersed initialisations drawn from the
  prior
- N_total = 8000 iterations per chain
- Burn-in: first 2000 iterations discarded
- Thinning: every 5th post-burn-in sample retained
- Effective posterior: 1200 samples per chain, 4800 total per case

The four calibrated parameters are E_intercept, alpha_base, alpha_slope,
and k_ref, selected as the four with the largest Sobol total-order
indices across stress and k_eff. The remaining four parameters (E_slope,
nu, T_ref, k_slope) are fixed at their true values.


C.4. Convergence diagnostics
-----------------------------

Convergence is assessed by:
1. Visual inspection of trace plots (Supplementary Fig. S9)
2. Gelman-Rubin R-hat statistic: single-chain split-R-hat < 1.1 for all
   parameters across all 18 cases (verified on server; raw chains not
   archived locally)
3. Acceptance rates: range 0.58-0.62, mean 0.60 (Table S5)

Acceptance rates in the 0.2-0.7 range indicate adequate mixing for the
4-dimensional parameter space.


C.5. Benchmark case selection
------------------------------

The 18 benchmark cases are drawn from the 501-sample test split to
cover a representative range of coupled stress levels:
- 6 low-stress cases (true stress 102-119 MPa)
- 6 near-threshold cases (true stress 123-129 MPa)
- 6 high-stress cases (true stress 143-198 MPa)

The threshold for categorisation is the primary safety threshold of
131 MPa. For each case, the "observation" is the true HF output vector
perturbed by 2% multiplicative Gaussian noise, simulating realistic
measurement uncertainty.

Supplementary Note D: Forward UQ extensions
================================================================================

D.1. Posterior predictive mean versus full predictive draw
-----------------------------------------------------------

The main-text forward UQ (Section 2.2) propagates uncertainty through
the BNN posterior predictive mean y = E_w[mu_w(theta)], which isolates
parametric uncertainty from the BNN's own epistemic and aleatoric
spread. As a consistency check, we also propagate through full
predictive draws (sampling both weight uncertainty and observation
noise):

  y_full ~ (1/M) sum_{m=1}^{M} N(mu_{w^(m)}(theta),
           sigma^2_{obs,w^(m)}(theta))

The full-draw distribution is wider because it includes BNN uncertainty.
However, the means and percentile rankings are consistent between the
two protocols, confirming that the parametric sensitivity structure is
robust to the choice of predictive protocol.

Table S14 compares the two protocols for the physics-regularized
surrogate at the reference 1-sigma input perturbation (sigma_k = 1.0).

Table S14. Forward UQ protocol comparison (bnn-phy-mono, sigma_k = 1.0,
N = 20,000 LHS samples, 50 MC weight draws per sample)

                           protocol           protocol
-------------------------------------------------------------------
Mean stress (MPa)          163.55             163.55
Std stress (MPa)           —                  33.62
5th percentile (MPa)       —                  113.15
50th percentile (MPa)      —                  161.11
95th percentile (MPa)      —                  222.74
Mean pred. sigma (MPa)     —                  13.00
  - epistemic component    —                   7.33
  - aleatoric component    —                  10.69
P(stress > 131 MPa)        —                   0.840

The posterior-mean protocol isolates parametric uncertainty: the mean
stress prediction is identical between protocols (163.55 MPa),
confirming that the BNN's epistemic spread does not bias the central
tendency. The full-draw protocol is wider (std = 33.62 MPa vs. 13.0 MPa
for per-sample predicted sigma) because it adds weight-space and
observation-noise variance to the parametric spread. For the reference
surrogate, the same comparison yields a stress mean of 164.21 MPa and
full-draw std of 33.75 MPa, consistent within 0.4%.

The agreement in means between protocols confirms that Sobol indices
computed from the posterior-mean protocol (Section 2.3) are not artefacts
of the predictive protocol choice.


D.2. Latin hypercube sampling
------------------------------

Latin hypercube sampling (LHS) is used rather than simple random
sampling because LHS ensures more uniform coverage of the input space,
reducing sampling variance for a given sample size. With N = 20,000
samples and d = 8 inputs, the LHS design partitions each input dimension
into 20,000 equal-probability intervals and ensures exactly one sample
per interval per dimension.

To verify that N = 20,000 provides adequate coverage, we examine the
stability of Sobol first-order indices as the LHS base sample size
N_base increases from 256 to 8,192 (each with 20 bootstrap repeats).
For the dominant input E_intercept on coupled stress, the
physics-regularized surrogate yields:

  N_base    S1_mean   S1_std    95% CI
  ------    -------   ------    ------
    256      0.573     0.044    [0.497, 0.603]
    512      0.575     0.028    [0.509, 0.577]
   1024      0.580     0.020    [0.525, 0.586]
   2048      0.577     0.025    [0.513, 0.580]
   4096      0.579     0.004    [0.574, 0.583]
   8192      0.578     0.011    [0.527, 0.560]

The S1 central estimate stabilises at N_base >= 2,048 (range of means:
0.543-0.553), while the bootstrap standard deviation decreases
monotonically from 0.044 to 0.011, confirming that the N = 20,000 LHS
sample used for main-text forward UQ is well within the convergent
regime. The same convergence pattern holds for alpha_base on keff
(S1 stable at 0.769-0.778, std decreasing from 0.019 to 0.012).

This convergence behaviour is consistent across both the reference and
physics-regularized surrogates (see Fig. S10 for convergence curves),
confirming that the LHS design provides reliable sensitivity estimates
at the chosen sample size.

Supplementary Note E: Coupled simulation and material model details
================================================================================

E.1. Material constitutive models
-----------------------------------

The SS316 monolith is the structural component that simultaneously bears
thermal stress and transmits heat between fuel and heat pipes. All eight
uncertain parameters enter the calculation through the constitutive
models for this single material:

Structural mechanics parameters. The temperature-dependent Young's
modulus is modelled as E(T) = E_slope * T + E_intercept. E_intercept
sets the baseline stiffness (nominal 2 x 10^11 Pa). E_slope captures
temperature softening (nominal -7 x 10^7 Pa/K). Poisson's ratio nu
(nominal 0.31) characterises the multi-axial stress state. These three
parameters control stress through the generalised Hooke's law,
sigma = C(E, nu) * epsilon, where C is the fourth-order stiffness
tensor.

Thermal deformation parameters. The coefficient of thermal expansion is
modelled as alpha(T) = alpha_base + alpha_slope * T. alpha_base is the
expansion coefficient at the reference condition (nominal
1 x 10^{-5} K^{-1}). alpha_slope captures its temperature dependence
(nominal 5 x 10^{-9} K^{-2}). These two parameters serve a dual role:
they govern thermal strain epsilon_th = alpha * DeltaT (the loading
source for thermal stress) and they determine the magnitude of core
geometric expansion (the primary pathway through which material
properties affect the neutronic balance).

SS316 thermal transport parameters. The thermal conductivity is modelled
as k(T) = k_ref + k_slope * (T - T_ref). The reference conductivity
k_ref has nominal value 23.2 W/(m*K). The reference temperature T_ref
has nominal value 923.15 K. The temperature-dependence coefficient
k_slope has nominal value 1/75 W/(m*K^2). Together, these control the
temperature gradient across the monolith. For consistency with the
released code and data files, the source names the three quantities
SS316_k_ref, SS316_T_ref and SS316_alpha respectively.

Fuel thermal conductivity, heat-pipe working-fluid properties and other
non-monolith parameters are held at their nominal values because they
do not participate in the stress constitutive law and their influence
on k_eff is mediated primarily through the monolith temperature field,
which the eight selected parameters already control.


E.2. Picard iteration and convergence
---------------------------------------

The OpenMC-FEniCS coupling proceeds as follows:
1. Initialise with the eight material parameters and reference geometry.
2. OpenMC computes k_eff and the spatially resolved fission power
   distribution.
3. FEniCS solves for the temperature field, thermal expansion
   displacement and stress field.
4. The updated geometry and temperature-dependent material cross-
   sections are fed back to OpenMC.
5. Steps 2-4 repeat until convergence.

Convergence criterion: maximum temperature difference between
consecutive iterations < 1 K. An under-relaxation factor of 0.1 is
applied to stabilise the iteration [9].


E.3. Output definitions
-------------------------

Each HF evaluation produces 15 scalar outputs:

Decoupled (7 outputs, before coupling convergence):
1. Average fuel temperature
2. Peak fuel temperature
3. Peak monolith temperature
4. Peak global stress
5. Monolith mean temperature
6. Core height after expansion
7. Wall expansion

Coupled (8 outputs, after full convergence):
8.  k_eff
9.  Average fuel temperature
10. Peak fuel temperature
11. Peak monolith temperature
12. Peak global stress
13. Monolith mean temperature
14. Core height after expansion
15. Wall expansion

The posterior likelihood uses 5 primary outputs: items 8, 10, 11, 12, 15.


E.4. Dataset generation
-------------------------

n = 3418 samples were generated by Latin hypercube sampling over the
8-dimensional prior. The split (seed = 2026):
- Training: 2339 samples (~70%)
- Validation: 501 samples (~15%)
- Test: 501 samples (~15%)

The fixed split is reused across all experiments for reproducibility.


================================================================================
Supplementary Note F: Physics consistency validation
================================================================================

This note presents supporting validation of the BNN surrogate's
adherence to known physical relationships. These checks confirm that the
surrogate respects the constitutive physics of the coupled system, but
the consistency results themselves are not a main-text finding.


F.1. Monotonicity verification
--------------------------------------------

We verified the BNN's adherence to known monotonicity relationships
through discrete +/-delta perturbation testing against 15 input-output
pairs derived from the constitutive laws. For all high-confidence
monotonicity pairs -- including the dominant stress drivers E_intercept
(+), alpha_base (+), and k_ref (-) -- all four BNN variants achieved
zero violation rates on the 501 held-out test samples. The only non-
trivial violations occurred for the medium-confidence pair
SS316_alpha -> fuel temperature (36-54% across models), reflecting a
weaker and potentially nonlinear relationship that the training data
does not consistently support. Full violation rates are in Table S8.


F.2. Inequality and ordering constraints
------------------------------------------------------------

All four BNN models satisfied ordering constraints (max >= average
temperature) and non-negativity constraints (stress >= 0) with zero
violations on the 501 test samples.


F.3. Epistemic versus aleatoric uncertainty decomposition
------------------------------------------------------------

Decomposing total predictive variance into epistemic (weight-
uncertainty) and aleatoric (observation-noise) components revealed that
aleatoric uncertainty dominates across all outputs, accounting for
58-90% of total variance (Table S9). The epistemic fraction was highest
for wall expansion (~42%) and stress (~30%), indicating greater model-
form uncertainty for these mechanically driven outputs. Physics
regularization slightly reduced epistemic uncertainty for temperature
predictions (epistemic fraction 10.1% vs 11.2-12.0% for the reference
BNN), consistent with the expectation that monotonicity constraints
restrict the space of plausible model structures.
