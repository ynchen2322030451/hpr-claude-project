Title:
Probabilistic neural surrogates for uncertainty-to-risk analysis in coupled
multi-physics systems: application to a heat-pipe-cooled reactor

概率神经代理模型在耦合多物理场系统不确定性-风险分析中的应用：
以热管冷却反应堆为例

ABSTRACT
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

We develop a probabilistic surrogate model for coupled thermo-mechanical
analysis of a heat-pipe-cooled reactor (HPR), enabling end-to-end uncertainty-
to-risk quantification at a fraction of the cost of high-fidelity simulation.
The surrogate, a heteroscedastic multilayer perceptron (HeteroMLP) trained on
~2900 coupled OpenMC–FEniCS simulations, predicts distributions over 15 physics
outputs across two fidelity levels—a decoupled prediction (iter1) and a converged
coupled steady-state (iter2)—from 8 material uncertainty inputs. A physics-
regularized variant (Level 2) embeds monotonicity constraints derived from domain
knowledge and achieves a test negative log-likelihood of 0.305, with stress
coefficient of determination R²=0.929 (RMSE=7.9 MPa). Forward propagation of
20,000 prior Monte Carlo samples reveals that multi-physics coupling substantially
compresses uncertainty: iter2 stress shifts 39 MPa below iter1 (mean), with
standard deviation reduced by 47%; keff uncertainty collapses by 183×. The
predicted probability of exceeding the 131 MPa design threshold is 0.847. Sobol
sensitivity analysis identifies SS316 steel thermal conductivity (SS316_k_ref) as
the dominant driver of stress (first-order index S₁=0.529) and thermal expansion
coefficient (alpha_base) as the dominant driver of keff (S₁=0.481), indicating
independent physical pathways. Observation-driven posterior calibration over 20
benchmark test cases demonstrates that the posterior predictive distribution
tightens around the observation and, for all 10 extreme-stress cases with observed
stress ≥ 220 MPa, the posterior safety fraction P_safe(131 MPa) converges to zero.
The surrogate achieves a single-sample inference latency of ~0.16 μs on GPU,
representing a speedup of over 23 million × versus the coupled high-fidelity solver.

2.1 Material Uncertainty Model

Eight material parameters are treated as uncertain inputs (Table 1):
E_slope, E_intercept (Young's modulus temperature dependence: E(T) = E_slope·T + E_intercept),
nu (Poisson's ratio), alpha_base, alpha_slope (thermal expansion: α(T) = alpha_base + alpha_slope·T),
SS316_T_ref, SS316_k_ref, SS316_alpha (SS316 steel thermal properties).
All parameters are assigned independent uniform priors covering plausible
manufacturing and material variability ranges.

八个材料参数被视为不确定性输入（见表1）……独立均匀先验分布……

2.2 Coupled High-Fidelity Simulation

The high-fidelity (HF) workflow couples OpenMC (Monte Carlo neutronics) with
FEniCS (finite element structural–thermal analysis) in an iterative feedback loop.
Starting from initial material parameters (iter1: decoupled prediction), the
neutronics calculation yields keff and power distribution, which drives the thermal
calculation for temperature fields, which in turn updates structural response.
Convergence (iter2: coupled steady-state) is reached when the keff and temperature
fields stabilize across iterations. Each evaluation takes approximately one hour on
a dedicated compute node.

A dataset of n ≈ 2900 samples was generated by Latin hypercube sampling of the
8-dimensional input space, covering the full prior uncertainty range. Each sample
provides both iter1 (first-pass, decoupled) and iter2 (converged coupled) outputs
across 15 physics quantities: 7 iter1 quantities (average fuel temperature,
maximum fuel temperature, maximum monolith temperature, maximum global stress,
monolith temperature, core height after expansion, wall expansion) and 8 iter2
quantities (keff plus the same 7 structural/thermal outputs).

高保真（HF）工作流将OpenMC（蒙特卡洛中子学）与FEniCS（有限元结构-热分析）以迭代
反馈方式耦合。iter1为解耦预测（第一轮，无反馈），iter2为收敛耦合稳态。每次评估
约需1小时。通过拉丁超立方采样生成约2900个样本……

2.3 Physics-Regularized Probabilistic Surrogate

The surrogate is a heteroscedastic multilayer perceptron (HeteroMLP) that receives
the 8-dimensional material parameter vector and simultaneously outputs a predictive
mean μ and log-variance log σ² for each of the 15 physics outputs. The training
objective for the baseline surrogate (Level 0) is the Gaussian negative log-
likelihood (NLL):

  L_NLL = Σ_i Σ_j [ (y_ij - μ_ij)² / (2σ_ij²) + (1/2) log σ_ij² ]

The physics-regularized variant (Level 2) augments this with a Spearman-rank-based
monotonicity regularization term that penalizes predicted rank correlations
inconsistent with known physical relationships (e.g., higher thermal conductivity
should reduce stress). Specifically, for each physically-expected monotone pair
(input p, output q), we compute the empirical Spearman correlation ρ̂_pq over a
mini-batch and penalize:

  L_mono = Σ_pairs max(0, δ - sign_pq · ρ̂_pq)

where sign_pq ∈ {+1, −1} encodes the expected sign of the correlation.

Hyperparameters for both Level 0 and Level 2 were independently optimized via
Optuna (40 trials per level, seed 2026) on the fixed validation set. The
train/val/test split (70%/15%/15%, n_train=2029, n_val=436, n_test=435) was
fixed prior to any model selection.

代理模型为异方差多层感知机（HeteroMLP）……Level 0基线以高斯NLL为损失，Level 2在此
基础上叠加基于Spearman秩相关的单调性正则化项，将已知的物理单调性关系嵌入训练……
超参数通过Optuna独立优化（每个层次40次试验，随机种子2026），训练/验证/测试划分
（约70%/15%/15%，n=2029/436/435）在模型选择之前固定……

2.4 Forward Uncertainty Propagation

20,000 samples are drawn from the joint prior distribution of the 8 input parameters.
For each sample, the surrogate returns predicted means μ(θ) for all 15 outputs.
The distribution of these predicted means constitutes the epistemic (model-mean)
forward UQ. The primary quantity of interest is the iter2 maximum global stress;
the design threshold is 131 MPa (main text; 110 and 120 MPa in Appendix S2).
The failure probability is estimated as P(stress > τ) = (1/N) Σ 1[μ_stress(θ_i) > τ].

前向不确定性传播：从8个输入参数的联合先验中抽取20000个样本，每个样本通过代理模型
得到μ(θ)，以此估计输出分布（模型均值，即认知不确定性）……主要关注量为iter2最大全局
应力；设计阈值131 MPa（正文），110/120 MPa见附录S2……

2.5 Global Sensitivity Analysis

First-order (S₁) and total-order (Sᵀ) Sobol indices are estimated using
Saltelli's extension of the quasi-random Sobol sequence (N=4096 base samples,
512 bootstrap replicates for 90% confidence intervals). Analysis is performed
separately for iter2 stress and iter2 keff, treating surrogate-mean predictions
as the function evaluator.

全局灵敏度分析：使用Saltelli方法估计一阶Sobol指数S₁和总阶指数Sᵀ（基础样本N=4096，
512次Bootstrap重采样估计90%置信区间）……

2.6 Observation-Driven Posterior Calibration

For 20 benchmark test cases drawn from the held-out test set (obs = true outputs
+ Gaussian observation noise), Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) with a Gaussian
likelihood is used to update the prior over 4 calibrated parameters
(E_intercept, alpha_base, alpha_slope, nu), holding the remaining 4 inputs
(E_slope, SS316_T_ref, SS316_k_ref, SS316_alpha) fixed at prior means. Each MCMC
run generates 1200 posterior samples (acceptance rate ≈ 0.40–0.53). The posterior
predictive safety fraction P_safe(τ) = P(predicted stress < τ | posterior) is
computed for τ = 131 MPa.

Separately, 10 extreme-stress benchmark cases with true observed stress ≥ 220 MPa
are included to test the behavior of the calibrated posterior in safety-relevant
extrapolation scenarios.

后验标定：对20个基准测试案例（来自保留测试集），以高斯似然为条件，对4个标定参数
（E_intercept, alpha_base, alpha_slope, nu）进行MCMC采样（其余4个参数固定为先验均值），
每次MCMC生成1200个后验样本（接受率约0.40–0.53）。计算后验安全分数P_safe(131 MPa)。
另行选取10个极端应力案例（真实应力≥220 MPa）进行外推验证……

3.1  Surrogate Accuracy and Physics Regularization
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────

【English】

The physics-regularized surrogate (Level 2) achieves a test NLL of 0.305, compared
to 0.359 for the baseline (Level 0), indicating improved distributional calibration
under the monotonicity-constrained training objective. Across the five primary
outputs (keff, max fuel temperature, max monolith temperature, maximum global stress,
wall expansion), the mean R² is 0.795 (Level 2) versus 0.790 (Level 0); the modest
aggregate improvement reflects that the regularization targets physical consistency
rather than raw accuracy.

Per-output analysis reveals more differentiated behavior. For the primary safety
metric, iter2 maximum global stress, both models achieve R²=0.929 with RMSE=7.9 MPa
and mean absolute error ≈ 6.1 MPa, with 90% prediction interval coverage (PICP₉₀)
of 0.913 (Level 2) and 0.931 (Level 0). For keff, Level 2 achieves R²=0.874 versus
0.865 for Level 0. Temperature outputs have lower R² (≈0.58–0.59 for both levels),
reflecting inherently lower sensitivity to the 8 material inputs in the coupled
steady-state regime; these outputs have correspondingly small absolute errors
(≤5 K RMSE). The wall expansion prediction achieves R²=0.996 in both models.

The practical advantage of Level 2 becomes apparent under distributional shift. In
out-of-distribution (OOD) evaluations using held-out samples with extreme alpha_base
values (thermal expansion coefficient extrapolation), the physics-regularized model
achieves iter2 stress R²=0.944, compared to R²=0.774 for the baseline—a gain of
Δ=+0.170 (Table 2). This gap directly reflects the enforcement of the alpha_base–
stress monotonicity relationship, which the baseline model may violate in
extrapolation. For E_intercept extrapolation, both models perform comparably
(R²=0.949 vs 0.940). These results support the choice of Level 2 for forward UQ
and sensitivity analysis.

3.2  Forward Uncertainty Propagation and Risk Quantification
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────

【English】

Forward propagation of 20,000 prior samples through the Level 2 surrogate reveals
that multi-physics coupling substantially reshapes the stress uncertainty distribution
(Figure 2).

In the decoupled prediction (iter1), where each simulation pass is evaluated without
neutronics–thermal feedback, the predicted maximum global stress has a mean of
192.7 MPa and a standard deviation of 40.9 MPa, with a 95th-percentile value of
264.8 MPa. The keff distribution is correspondingly broad: mean 1.1025, standard
deviation 0.0625 (approximately 6250 pcm spread).

The coupled steady-state (iter2) compresses both distributions markedly. The iter2
stress mean is 153.4 MPa—a reduction of 39.3 MPa (20%) relative to iter1—and the
standard deviation decreases from 40.9 to 21.7 MPa (a 47% reduction). The 95th
percentile drops from 264.8 to 189.3 MPa. The physical mechanism is straightforward:
under the converged multi-physics coupling, materials with high thermal expansion
coefficients (which would produce high stress in decoupled analysis) also expand
the core geometry, reducing neutron leakage and raising keff, which reduces power
density and moderates temperature—thereby partially offsetting the stress. This
negative feedback is absent in the decoupled prediction.

The keff distribution compression is more dramatic: the iter2 standard deviation
is 3.42 × 10⁻⁴ (34 pcm), compared to 0.0625 in iter1—a reduction of approximately
183×. The physical interpretation is that iterative coupling drives keff toward a
narrow equilibrium band determined primarily by thermal balance, regardless of the
specific material parameter realization.

The primary risk metric, P(iter2 stress > 131 MPa), is estimated at 0.847 under
the prior (epistemic, mean-only). This indicates that under the assumed material
uncertainty distributions, the design threshold is expected to be exceeded in a
large fraction of realizations. The predicted stress distribution has a long upper
tail (max observed ≈ 229 MPa), consistent with the wide material uncertainty range.

3.3  Global Sensitivity Analysis
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────

【English】

Sobol sensitivity analysis identifies distinct dominant pathways for the two primary
quantities of interest (Figure 3, Table 3).

For iter2 maximum global stress (Level 2), the first-order Sobol index for SS316
steel thermal conductivity (SS316_k_ref) is S₁=0.529 (90% CI: 0.516–0.542), more
than twice the next contributor. E_intercept (the temperature-independent Young's
modulus component) ranks second with S₁=0.202 (CI: 0.180–0.225), followed by
SS316_T_ref with S₁=0.128 (CI: 0.109–0.148). All three have well-separated,
non-zero confidence intervals and account for approximately 86% of the first-order
variance decomposition. The remaining five inputs each have S₁ < 0.07, with alpha_base
at S₁=0.052 (CI: 0.028–0.076) and alpha_slope, nu, E_slope, and SS316_alpha having
intervals that include or are near zero.

The dominance of SS316_k_ref over stress can be understood physically: the steel
thermal conductivity controls how efficiently heat is transported from the fuel
to the heat pipe, thereby setting the local temperature gradient and the resulting
thermal stress. High conductivity reduces temperature gradients and mechanical
stress; low conductivity concentrates heat and amplifies stress.

For iter2 keff (Level 2), alpha_base (the temperature-independent thermal expansion
coefficient) is the single dominant factor with S₁=0.481 (CI: 0.466–0.495), followed
by alpha_slope (S₁=0.065, CI: 0.042–0.088). The remaining parameters have CI
intervals that include zero and are not interpreted as stable contributors.
The dominance of alpha_base reflects the mechanism described above: thermal expansion
controls core geometry and thereby neutron leakage, the principal driver of keff
variation in this design.

These results confirm that the two primary outputs are controlled by independent
physical pathways: steel thermal transport properties dominate stress, while thermal
expansion governs reactivity. This independence has direct implications for safety
design: improving steel conductivity uniformity primarily reduces stress risk without
substantially affecting keff behavior.

Comparing Level 0 and Level 2 Sobol results, the baseline surrogate assigns
E_intercept as the dominant stress factor (S₁=0.711), while Level 2 assigns SS316_k_ref.
This difference likely reflects the monotonicity constraints that prevent Level 2 from
over-attributing variance to elastic modulus at the expense of thermal conductivity,
which has a stronger physical connection to thermal stress in this system. The Level 2
Sobol results are presented as primary due to the demonstrated OOD robustness of
the physics-regularized model.

⚠️  NOTE for paper: The Sobol interpretation for Level 2 is data-supported (CI
does not include zero for top-3 stress factors). The Level 0 vs Level 2 discrepancy
in dominant factor (E_intercept vs SS316_k_ref) should be acknowledged; claim that
Level 2 is more physically reasonable but do not overstate mechanism interpretation.

输入S₁均小于0.07，置信区间接近或包含零，不构成稳定主控因素。

SS316_k_ref对应力的主导可从物理机制理解：钢的热导率决定燃料到热管的热量传递效率，
进而控制局部温度梯度和热应力。导率高则温度梯度小、应力低；导率低则热量集中、应力大。

对iter2 keff（Level 2），alpha_base（热膨胀系数温度无关项）为唯一主控因素，S₁=0.481
（CI: 0.466–0.495），alpha_slope次之（S₁=0.065，CI: 0.042–0.088）。其余参数置信区间
包含零，不作为稳定贡献者解读。alpha_base的主导反映上述机制：热膨胀控制堆芯几何，
进而决定中子泄漏，是该设计keff变化的主要驱动因素。

这些结果表明两个主要输出受独立物理路径控制：钢的热传导性质主控应力，热膨胀主控反应性。
这一独立性对安全设计有直接启示：提高钢导率均匀性主要降低应力风险，对keff行为影响较小。

比较Level 0与Level 2的Sobol结果：基线代理将E_intercept列为应力主控因素（S₁=0.711），
而Level 2列为SS316_k_ref。这一差异可能反映单调性约束阻止了Level 2将方差过度归因于
弹性模量，而物理上热导率对热应力的联系更为直接。鉴于Level 2在OOD测试中展现出更好的
泛化性，以Level 2的Sobol结果作为主要分析结果。

【English】

MCMC posterior calibration was performed on 20 benchmark test cases covering a range
of observed stress from approximately 100 to 226 MPa. For each case, four material
parameters (E_intercept, alpha_base, alpha_slope, nu) were updated by conditioning
on the observed outputs (stress and keff), yielding 1200 posterior samples per case
with acceptance rates in the range 0.40–0.53. (Results in this section are based on
the available benchmark case posterior samples; a complete contraction summary from
the current model run is pending.)

For cases with low observed stress (obs < 115 MPa, e.g., cases 4 and 10 with obs
stress 114.4 and 102.0 MPa), the posterior mean surrogate prediction closely tracks
the observation, with posterior stress predictions of 129.3 and 139.3 MPa respectively.
For cases near the design threshold (obs ≈ 131 MPa), the posterior locates predictions
within a few MPa of the observation.

The posterior safety fraction P_safe(131 MPa) = P(predicted stress < 131 MPa |
posterior) provides a risk statement conditioned on the available measurement. For
cases with low observed stress, P_safe is close to 1.0; for cases with intermediate
stress (obs ≈ 130–145 MPa), P_safe ranges from 0.13 to 0.85, reflecting residual
uncertainty in the remaining fixed parameters; for high-stress cases, P_safe
approaches zero.

The extreme-stress validation (10 cases with true stress ≥ 220 MPa) demonstrates a
consistent pattern: for all 10 cases, the posterior probability of exceeding the
131 MPa threshold converges to P_exceed = 1.0, while the prior probability ranges
from 0.84 to 0.96 (Table 4). The posterior mean surrogate prediction closely tracks
the true stress in these cases (mean absolute error < 6 MPa), demonstrating that the
surrogate can accurately locate parameter regions consistent with extreme observations.
The Sobol analysis (Section 3.3) indicates that these high-stress cases are driven by
low SS316_k_ref values, and the calibration systematically shifts the posterior
toward this region.

These results illustrate the value of the surrogate-based calibration pipeline: a
measurement indicating high stress not only confirms the risk assessment but sharpens
it—the posterior P_safe = 0 is more actionable than the prior P_safe ≈ 0.05–0.16.

calibrated parameters. The 10-case extreme-stress benchmark uses a slightly different
(possibly extended) calibration setup. Full contraction statistics for the current
model run are not yet available from a dedicated summary CSV; individual posterior

【English — key points for draft】

4.1 Physics coupling as an uncertainty modulator

The 47% reduction in stress standard deviation from iter1 to iter2 is not simply
a model artifact—it reflects a genuine negative feedback loop in the coupled physics.
This finding has a practical implication for safety analysis: performing UQ at the
decoupled level (iter1) would overestimate stress uncertainty by roughly a factor
of 2 in standard deviation, and would overestimate tail risk (P > 131 MPa prior
probability in uncoupled analysis would be higher). The surrogate's ability to
simultaneously model both iter1 and iter2 outputs enables this comparison at low cost.

4.2 The case for physics regularization

The Level 2 vs Level 0 comparison in the main evaluation (in-distribution, test set)
shows only modest aggregate improvement. However, the OOD evaluation for alpha_base
(Δ R² = +0.170) demonstrates a concrete benefit in scenarios where material parameters
fall outside the training distribution—a realistic concern for reactor materials under
extended operation. The monotonicity regularization does not force the model to be
exactly monotone, but it biases the learned function toward physically consistent
behavior in data-sparse regions.

The change in Sobol dominant factor (E_intercept in L0 vs SS316_k_ref in L2) is
noteworthy. While a definitive physical argument is difficult to make from Sobol
analysis alone, the SS316_k_ref interpretation is mechanistically more direct for
thermal stress. We present both results in Appendix S3 and note that the Level 2
assignment is more consistent with physical expectations.

4.3 Computational efficiency

The ~23 million× speedup versus the coupled HF solver enables UQ analyses that
would otherwise be computationally prohibitive. Specifically: 20,000 forward UQ
samples require ~3 ms on GPU (versus ~23,000 CPU-hours for HF), and the 1200-sample
MCMC for a single case completes in ~0.2 ms (versus ~1200 HF evaluations). This
efficiency enables iterative design exploration, including sensitivity analysis
and parameter uncertainty sweep, within a single interactive session.

4.4 Limitations

The surrogate was trained on a fixed dataset generated from a specific HPR design
point. Extrapolation beyond the training distribution (as tested in OOD evaluation)
shows degraded but acceptable performance for Level 2. The MCMC calibration fixes
4 of the 8 input parameters at prior means; in practice, all 8 parameters may be
uncertain simultaneously, requiring either a full 8-dimensional calibration (higher
MCMC cost) or marginalizing over the fixed parameters.

The nearest-neighbor HF proxy validation (Section 2.6) is a weaker form of
validation than a true high-fidelity rerun at the posterior mean; the latter would
require ~5 HF evaluations × ~1 h each, which was beyond the scope of the current
study. The proxy validation (mean stress error at posterior = 31.6 MPa vs prior
33.2 MPa) provides a conservative estimate of calibration benefit.

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

【English】

This work presents a probabilistic surrogate framework for coupled multi-physics
uncertainty quantification in a heat-pipe-cooled reactor, demonstrating four
principal findings:

1. Physics regularization improves generalization. The Level 2 heteroscedastic MLP,
   trained with Spearman-rank monotonicity constraints, matches in-distribution
   accuracy (test NLL 0.305, stress R²=0.929) while showing substantially better
   OOD performance for alpha_base extrapolation (R² 0.774→0.944). This supports
   embedding domain knowledge as a soft regularizer rather than a hard physics
   constraint.

2. Multi-physics coupling compresses uncertainty. Forward propagation reveals that
   coupled steady-state outputs have significantly tighter distributions than
   decoupled first-pass predictions: 47% stress standard deviation reduction and
   183× keff uncertainty compression. These are consequences of the neutronics–
   thermal feedback mechanism and would not be captured by single-physics surrogates.

3. Distinct sensitivity pathways exist for stress and keff. Sobol analysis
   identifies SS316_k_ref as the dominant stress driver (S₁=0.529) and alpha_base
   as the dominant keff driver (S₁=0.481), indicating that design interventions for
   stress reduction and reactivity control are largely independent.

4. Posterior calibration sharpens risk assessments. MCMC-based parameter updating
   conditioned on observations converges P_exceed(131 MPa) to 1.0 for all extreme-
   stress scenarios, demonstrating that the surrogate pipeline correctly identifies
   high-risk material parameter regions from observational evidence.

The computational speedup (>23 million ×) makes this framework practical for
iterative design exploration and real-time risk monitoring in reactor systems with
significant material uncertainty.
